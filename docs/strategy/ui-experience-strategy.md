# UI Experience Strategy

## Purpose

This file stores the approved visual and page-experience standards for clayduncan.com. It is limited to UI, UX, layout, visual language, and component behavior.

## Experience Goal

The site should feel premium, credible, warm, modern, local, and easy to scan. It should support serious mortgage decisions with calm confidence while still feeling personal and human.

Important service pages should feel like complete landing experiences, not plain SEO articles. Visitors should understand the page subject, audience, proof, and next step quickly without feeling pushed through a generic funnel.

The approved visual model for service landing pages is **Concept A · Warm Modern Authority**, first selected from `/concepts/va-a/` and applied to `/huntsville/va-loans/`.

## Visual Direction

- Use a refined editorial style rather than a conventional mortgage-template look.
- Keep the experience content-first, static, fast, and restrained.
- Make the first viewport immediately signal the page topic and brand.
- Use real photography and grounded local imagery whenever possible.
- Use practical, human visuals over abstract illustrations.
- Use visual hierarchy, spacing, and proof modules to create polish.
- Prefer clean structure and confident typography over decorative effects.
- Keep pages trustworthy, high-quality, and approachable rather than flashy.
- Treat the Clay banner/background style as the visual north star for the personal site; see `docs/strategy/ui-brand-handoff.md`.
- Preserve the Clay hero image treatment with the warm modern interior background at minimum unless a stronger real-life equivalent replaces it.
- Build new service landing pages from the warm modern authority system: off-white editorial base, strong serif headings, clean sans UI, deep navy CTAs, cognac accents, and real human/local imagery.

## Premium Visual Standard

Premium should mean:

- Clear hierarchy with no visual clutter.
- Confident first-screen composition.
- Strong but controlled typography.
- Thoughtful white space.
- Real imagery that supports the page topic.
- Proof and compliance details integrated calmly.
- Purposeful color accents instead of busy decoration.
- Components that feel designed, not assembled.
- Mobile layouts that feel intentionally composed rather than collapsed.

Premium should not mean:

- Overly glossy marketing sections.
- Generic stock-photo styling.
- Decorative effects that distract from the offer.
- Dense card walls.
- Dark tech/futuristic styling unless the page subject truly calls for it.

## Page Experience Standards

- Every high-priority landing page should have a clear hero, visible next step, proof, structured explanation, local context, FAQs when appropriate, source panel when claims depend on official program information, and final CTA.
- The first viewport should include a hint of the next section on both desktop and mobile when feasible.
- Pages should be easy to scan under time pressure.
- Use direct section headings and compact supporting copy.
- Avoid long uninterrupted text blocks.
- Keep CTAs practical and specific to the page intent.
- Use source and compliance information as trust builders, not as clutter.
- Keep layout density balanced: rich enough to feel complete, spacious enough to breathe.

## Landing Page Pattern

Baseline pattern for service landing pages:

1. Editorial hero with audience, location, topic, and primary CTA.
2. Proof strip or trust cues near the hero.
3. Direct-answer section with a clear answer-style heading.
4. Three to five structured explanation cards for the main decision factors.
5. Local-context section that visually proves relevance to Huntsville, Madison, Redstone Arsenal, or North Alabama when relevant.
6. Process or readiness section that helps the visitor understand what happens next.
7. FAQ section using visible, real questions and direct answers.
8. Official source panel when page claims rely on program or government information.
9. Final CTA matching the visitor’s likely next step.

The VA Loans page is the first approved baseline for this richer landing-page direction. `Concept A · Warm Modern Authority` is the selected visual model for service landing pages.

Visual baseline details:

- Warm off-white page base.
- Asymmetric editorial hero with large serif headline.
- Real image or approved Clay/background asset on the right.
- Compact callout overlay using cognac accenting.
- Proof strip directly below/near the hero using warm cognac top rules.
- Direct-answer section immediately after the first viewport.
- White cards over warm neutral section bands.
- Navy emphasis/process band with cognac details.

## Layout Patterns

- Use wide, editorial sections rather than nested cards.
- Use cards for individual repeated items, proof snippets, FAQs, and compact panels.
- Do not put cards inside cards.
- Use full-width bands or clean constrained sections for page rhythm.
- Use asymmetric hero layouts when imagery can support the page.
- Pair large headlines with short, readable supporting copy.
- Keep repeated grids stable with clear responsive behavior.
- Use proof strips for compact trust signals such as local relevance, process cues, reviews, or NMLS-related confidence.
- Use split sections for local context, quotes, source panels, or next-step prompts.

## Typography Guidance

- Use strong serif typography for major editorial headings.
- Use sans-serif typography for navigation, CTAs, labels, proof metadata, and compact UI text.
- Use Source Serif 4 for the primary editorial heading voice.
- Use Inter for body copy and UI text.
- Use Source Serif 4 for h1, h2, h3, proof numerals, major quote text, and major callout headings.
- Use Inter for paragraph copy, navigation, buttons, labels, metadata, card body text, and compliance/footer text.
- Interior service pages should use powerful but controlled headline sizes.
- Avoid homepage-scale headline treatment on every interior page.
- Keep labels, eyebrow text, and metadata crisp and consistent.
- Do not use negative letter spacing.
- Do not scale font size directly with viewport width beyond controlled responsive clamps.
- Make sure button and card text fits cleanly on mobile and desktop.

## Color Guidance

Approved core direction:

- Warm off-white page background: `#f4f1ea`.
- Deep navy for trust, structure, navigation, and body emphasis.
- Confident blue for primary actions and active signals.
- Cognac / warm wood as the main premium accent.
- Muted sage/olive as the quiet secondary accent.
- Princeton lime only as a rare, tiny energy accent.
- Black or near-black for primary heading weight.
- Muted neutrals for panels, dividers, and secondary text.

Use color with restraint. The site should not become a one-note palette, a blue-only document, or a Princeton-lime-heavy interface. Bright blue should mostly signal actions, links, or important interactive emphasis.

Brand palette direction should be personal-brand first and Princeton-compatible. The banner/background assets support a warm modern palette: off-white, charcoal, trust navy, action blue, warm wood/cognac, muted sage/olive, and only tiny Princeton-lime accents.

Current approved token direction:

- `#f4f1ea` warm off-white page base.
- `#141414` primary ink/headline.
- `#24305f` trust navy and primary CTA fill.
- `#2c6ef6` action blue, used selectively.
- `#b17845` cognac/warm wood accent.
- `#8a522c` dark cognac hover/accent.
- `#697845` muted sage/olive support.
- `#eef1e5` soft sage surface.
- Princeton lime should remain rare and should not drive major page sections.

CTA color rules:

- Primary CTAs use deep navy fill with white text.
- Primary CTA hover uses dark cognac.
- Secondary CTAs use outlined navy.
- Secondary CTA hover uses dark cognac for border/text.
- Avoid bright-blue filled CTAs on warm authority pages unless there is a deliberate reason.

Avoid:

- Purple or purple-blue gradient dominance.
- Dark neon tech styling.
- Beige-heavy or overly brown palettes.
- Decorative orbs, bokeh blobs, or generic gradient backgrounds.
- Low-contrast text or pale CTAs.

## Visual Asset Guidance

- Prefer real, relevant images over abstract SVGs or generic illustrations.
- Hero images should reveal something meaningful: person, place, work context, community, homebuying context, training setting, or local environment.
- Avoid dark, blurred, overly cropped, or atmospheric images when the visitor needs clarity.
- Use image overlays sparingly and only when they clarify the page topic.
- For AI training pages, visuals should feel like practical field training, not futuristic AI hype.
- For VA loan and local mortgage pages, visuals should feel grounded, trustworthy, local, and human.
- Use `public/images/Clay-Duncan-wbackground.jpg` or a responsive crop of the same visual treatment as the primary Clay homepage hero reference.
- Use the warm interior background as atmosphere and first-impression support, not as a repeated wallpaper across the whole site.

## Component Standards

- Buttons should be obvious, accessible, and consistent.
- Primary CTAs should use deep navy fill, not bright blue, on the warm authority system.
- Secondary CTAs should be outlined or quieter.
- Feature cards should explain real decision factors, not decorative blurbs.
- FAQ cards should be simple, readable, and direct.
- Source panels should be compact and credible.
- Proof strips should use real signals or program-relevant cues with a cognac top-rule accent.
- Local context panels should make geography and use case obvious.
- Process/timeline sections should be scannable in four or fewer major steps when possible.
- Callouts should clarify or reinforce the page, not decorate it, and should use cognac/warm accents before blue/lime.

## Mobile Experience

- Mobile layouts should feel designed, not merely stacked.
- Prevent horizontal overflow.
- Keep hero text readable without crowding the viewport.
- Hide or simplify decorative overlays on small screens when they would create clutter.
- Keep CTAs easy to tap and visually distinct.
- Preserve the page’s scan path: hero, proof, answer, explanation, local/process, FAQ, CTA.
- Make sure no text overlaps imagery, buttons, cards, or neighboring sections.
- Keep section spacing generous enough for readability but compact enough to maintain momentum.

## Things To Avoid

- Plain walls of text.
- Generic mortgage-template visuals.
- Fake dashboard or app-interface gimmicks.
- Abstract robot, AI brain, or futuristic imagery for AI-related pages.
- Overusing identical layouts across every landing page.
- Drifting back into a Princeton blue/lime-heavy palette.
- Bright-blue filled CTAs as the default on warm authority pages.
- Burying CTAs, proof, source links, or compliance signals.
- Hero sections that do not state the audience and page subject quickly.
- Oversized interior headlines that create awkward wrapping.
- Nested cards or decorative card-heavy sections.
- Visual clutter from too many badges, pills, shadows, or panels.
- Text overlapping images or other UI elements.
- Adding extra frameworks or client-side UI complexity for static landing pages.
